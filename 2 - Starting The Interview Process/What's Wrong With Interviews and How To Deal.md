# There Is A Lot Wrong With The Interview Process...How To Best Handle That

For those who have interviewed before, it's apparent that there are a lot of issues with the interview process. This section will cover the main issues, and how best to deal with those.

## Issues With Coding Interviews

### It Doesn't Reflect The Work You Do On A Day-To-Day Basis

You're not doing LeetCode style problems at work. However, these are common in technical interviews. This leads to some weird phenomenon:

- Recent grads actually have an advantage, since the material that gets tested in LeetCode-style problems is common in college CS programs. For those who have been in the industry for some time, they're actually at a disadvantage. As a quick workaround, those who have been in the industry for a while should pay special attention to the material they went over in school; it will be useful when they encounter LeetCode-style problems in their interviews.
- Regardless of where you are in your career, it is imperitive to practice (even if it's just LeetCode questions).

### Bad Questions Prioritizing Memorization

A lot of questions asked are in these three categories:

- Focusing on obscure or academic topics, not topics frequently encountered in the workplace.
- The questions rely on a "gotcha" moment. Not all good engineers can quickly reach that "gotcha" moment under the pressure of an interview.
- The questions and interview process lend themselves to be memorized, not understood. It doesn't help that those who encounted coding questions in previous interviews are more likely to pass interviews. At the very least, this should be your call to practice, practice, and practice some more.

### A Lot Of Interviewers Are Bad

Most companies just draft their engineering staff to run interviews. Not all engineers have the incentive to give a good interview. Sometimes they're just there because they have to and they will work on other things during the interview (or, at the very least, they are distracted because of their other work obligations).

This leads to these phenomena:

- Bad interviewers are inclined to not judge closely. Since no one wants to hire a dud, they'll likely default to rejecting the candidate.
- Bad interviewers are less involved, leading to less one-on-one interaction between interviewer and candidate. This is really bad, because a major part of a coding interview is to understand the candidate's thought process.

### Interviewing Is More Of A Numbers Game

Even good engineers are bad at being consistently good in interviews. According to the staff at `interviewing.io`, only a quarter of people are consistent from interview to interview. Most aren't consistently good or bad; they're all over the place.

### Vicious Cycles

The nature of coding interviews begets an interview prep industry. Companies retool their questions with that in mind, so candidates are incentivized to double down on interview prep, expanding the prep industry.

## Possible Issues With The Interview Process At Large

### Recruiter Incentives

Both third-party and in-house recruiters have differing incentives from each other and from you.

In general, third-party recruiters are a mixed bag. By and large, their incentive is to get a deal done, not getting you the best deal. Because of the cut that recruiting firms get, they're not into negotiating your offer. They would rather settle as many deals as quickly as possible.

In-house recruiters have slightly different incentives. They're trained to mobilize if prospects are likely to go to similarly-paying firms and make offers within specific bands (ideally less than what you wanted).

## How To Overcome

### Both Thought Process And Optimal Solutions Are Equally Important Now

In the past, while optimal solutions were important in coding interviews, the process was counted more. Now, standards have increased, and both are now equally important.

Ideally, you'll have both drilled down. However, in less optimal cases, you can handle the optimal solution part of the equation by practicing problems and you can handle the process part by ensuring you have good communication skills and -- more importantly -- by ensuring you keep an open line of communication with the interviewers so they can listen to your thought process.

### The First Twenty Minutes Are The Most Important

The good news is that first impressions aren't the end-all be-all for interviews. The bad news is that the first twenty minutes are. By the twenty minute mark of an interview, you're likely to have:

- Made your first impressions
- Started working on the problem. This will involve asking clarifying questions, breaking the problem down, and implementing. Assuming introductions take five minutes, that gives interviewers fifteen minutes to get an initial feel for how you work and think.

### Focus On The Technical Part More Than The Communication Part

If you're communication skills are servicable enough, there are diminishing returns on your performance if you keep improving it. However, there aren't similar trends for technical skills. In other words, you should have good communication skills, but your technical skills should be better.

### Practice, Practice, Practice

There is a direct correlation between how many prior interviews a candidate has taken and their likelihood to pass an interview. In other words, whether through real or mock interviews, get practice.

### You'll Need To Treat Applications As A Numbers Game

Based off what I mentioned above, and given the other parts of the application process (cover letters, resumes, the actual application, etc.), there are a lot of things out of your control. While you must improve your skills, be mindful that interviewing is a numbers game as much as it is a skills based endeavour.
