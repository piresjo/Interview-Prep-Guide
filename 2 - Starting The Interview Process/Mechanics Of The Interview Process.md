# Mechanics Of The Interview Process

From *Beyond Cracking The Coding Interview*

## Note On Centralized Vs. Decentralized

Centralized interviews have you applying to a specific level only. Once you get far enough in the interview process (usually when done), that's when you'll start talking to speific teams. If you've applied to Meta or Amazon, you should be familiar with this.

Decentralized interviews have you applying to a specific team. You're talking with them from the beginning of the process. You can do this with multiple teams (thus helping your chances of getting in somewhere at the company) and you're likely to know your hiring manager (which can help with negotiations).

## Hiring Process Inverted Pyramid

It is helpful to represent the hiring process (and the chances you have to get to the next stage) as an inverted pyramid

```
|                   Source/Apply                   | 10%
    |               Recruiter Call            |      75%
        |           Tech Screen          |           25%
            |         Onsite        |                33%
                |     Offer    |                     33%
                    | Hire |
```

## Each Step Of The Process

### Recruiter Call

You're likely to encounter a recruiter call during your interview process (either after applying or maybe they're reaching out to you). These don't matter much for the outcome, but it's always good to have a good first impression. That said, here are three things that could hurt you during this part of the process:

- Act uninterested in the role or being rude - To deal with this, don't be rude and indicate interest in the company and role
- Communicate poorly
- Not meeting the job requirements

Even if there is a mismatch between what they're looking for and your experience, you could present some things in your past experience that satisfy what they want. For instance, if someone is looking for experience in startups, but your experience is mostly in big companies, you can mention moments in those companies where you acted like you had startup experience.

Don't disclose too many details about where you are in the job search. The recruiter should not know compensation history, compensation expectations, and where you are in the job hunt.

Make sure you ask your recruiter meaningful questions about how the rest of the process will play out.

### Technical Phone Screen

There is fairly low pass rate at this stage, given how expensive onsites can be. The good news is that the pass rates go up from here.

Coding questions are most likely to be asked. Some comapnies go more towards a Data Structures and Algorithms approach in their questions; others deemphasize those in lieu of more practical questions (there will be multiple sections covering coding questions).

You should be allotted some time to ask some questions. Most interviewers would use questions for their final decision. Out of the remaining ones, half expect questions to be asked, and the other half expect both questions to be asked

To play it safe. Ask questions, and make sure that they indicate that you're interested in the company and role.

### The Onsite

These nowadays can be in-person or virtual. For the most part, the rules apply to both kinds of onsites.

These interviews delve deeper into how you are as an engineer, as well as determining how you are as an employee in general.

Usually, onsites have one or more rounds of:  

- Coding (Anywhere from 1-4, usually 2-3)
- System Design (1 or 2)
- Behavioral (1 or 2)
    - These might be conducted by hiring managers, although some companies have engineers doing it.

If you're having an onsite from a company that does decentralized hiring, you'll likely be interviewed by the team.

### Team Matching

If not, this is where the team matching section comes in. You will have interviews with multiple hiring managers for multiple teams. If there is agreement on both ends, there is a match.

## Good Questions To Ask

### Questions To Avoid

- Asking about work/life balance - It comes off like you don't want to work. There are related questions you can ask that will tell you about work/life balance that make you look better.
- Questions For HR
- Time fillers/personal questions

### Team Caliber/Culture

- How long have you been there?
- What work are you doing?
- What was the most interesting/favorite project you worked on here?
- How often have you moved from team to team?
    - Note - We're trying to find out how frequently people switch teams. Not everyone is reassigned out of their own volition, and some people might not like being switched from one team to another.

### Engineering

- What is your stack?
- What is the reason for the stack?
- What is test coverage/infrastructure/monitoring like?
- How is a typical day for you?
- What portion of time is spent on new stuff vs iteration?
- What tools do you use?

## Attrition
